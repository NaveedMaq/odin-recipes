Yakhni

Description

An authentic Kashmiri dish, Kashmiri Mutton Yakhni is best served for lunch or dinner with jeera rice or pulao. It is usually served at the end of the wazwaan to give a slight sour taste to the taste buds. It is a single large ball of meat and fat

The origin of the Kashmir's ethnic/heritage cuisine—wazwan—dates back to the 14th century when the Mongol ruler Timur invaded India in 1348 during the reign of Nasiuddin Muhammad of the Tughlaq dynasty, who brought skilled persons of various professions including cooks from Samarkand to Kashmir. These skilled cooks, called wazas in the local language, cooked meat into various dishes and classified them according to shape and gravies used for their preparation. The credit for popularizing wazwan dishes goes to Persian and Sanskrit immigrants. The term waazi is basically a Sanskrit term waja, which means to cook. The origin of the term has also been derived from a Persian word, ashpaaz, which means a special cook proficient in making soups, and with the passage of time the term became waza

The Kashmiri wazwan has been influenced by various cultural migrants that have swept into Kashmir from time to time. The names of the wazwan dishes reflect Persian, Turkish, Arabic, and Central Asian influences. The term kabab is Arabic in origin, Korma has Turkish roots, and rogan josh, yakhni, aab gosh, rista, and goshtaba stem from Persian sources. Various spices are used for the preparation and processing of wazwan: shonth (dried ginger), dalchini (cinnamon), elaichi (cardamom), laung (cloves), badyan (fennel), etc. The spices used in its preparation give special taste and aroma and suggest its Sanskrit influence

Ingredients

  • 500 g Mutton chopped
  • 400 g Curd
  • 4 Cardamoms
  • 2 Cloves
  • 1/2 inch Cinnamon stick
  • 1 tsp Dry Ginger Powder
  • 1 to 2 tbsp Aniseed Powder
  • 2 tbsp Mint Leaves
  • Salt as per taste

Steps to Prepare

  1. Pressure cook the mutton with 1 1/2 cups of water and a pinch fo salt for 20 to 30 minutes or until 3/4ths cooked.
  2. Reserve the water and keep the mutton pieces aside.
  3. Heat curd in a pan and stir continously to prevent it from splitting.
  4. Add the cardamoms, cloves, cinnamons and stir for another 10 minutes
  5. Add the ginger powder and stir a minute or two more.
  6. Add the aniseed powder and add the mutton pieces with the water.
  7. Gently mix well.
  8. Add the mint leaves and reduce flame to low.
  9. Simmer for 20 to 30 minutes, stirring from time to time.
  10. Cover and cook for 3 hours.
  11. Serve hot with jeera rice or a simple pulao.
